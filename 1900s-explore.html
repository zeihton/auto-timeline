The birth of mass mobility in the United States

Ford Model T: The Game Changer

Introduced in 1908, the Ford Model T revolutionized transportation. It was affordable, reliable, and built on the first moving assembly line — making it accessible to the middle class.

---------------- Life in the 1900s ----------------

Economy and Work Culture


In 1903, Henry Ford founded Ford Motor Company and began reshaping the economy. His groundbreaking decision to pay workers $5 a day in 1914 was more than generosity — it gave his own employees the purchasing power to buy the cars they built, which drastically reduced employee turnover.

Ford also pioneered vertical integration: he owned rubber plantations, steel mills, and even railroads — controlling everything from raw materials to final production.

Roads and Urbanization


In the early 1900s, America’s roads were mainly muddy trails, suited more for horses than cars. Most were impassable after rain.

Car owners, farmers, and bicycle clubs advocated for better roads. This led to the Federal Aid Road Act of 1916, marking the beginning of the U.S. federal government's involvement in road building.

Interestingly, the push for “Good Roads” initially came from bicyclists, not motorists.

Impact on American Families


The automobile became more than just a machine — it was a gateway to freedom. For many rural families, cars reduced isolation and improved access to healthcare, education, and social life.

For families who couldn’t afford a car, they became aspirational symbols — signs of independence, self-reliance, and modernity.

Environmental Effects


Ironically, early cars were considered environmentally friendly compared to horse-drawn transport. In cities, horses left behind manure and often died in the street.

But cars brought new issues: air pollution, oil dependency, and sprawling development. In 1900, few imagined the long-term environmental consequences of the internal combustion engine.
