The Automobile in the Age of War (1914–1945)

When cars became weapons — and women, workers.

World War I (1914–1918)

During WWI, automobiles were used for the first time on a massive scale in warzones: ambulances, troop transport, and even armored cars replaced horse-drawn wagons.

Early Mass Mobilization: The U.S. Army purchased over 12,000 trucks and cars during WWI, helping to shift perceptions of cars as utility machines, not just luxuries.

Technical innovation under pressure: Military need accelerated advancements in engines, durability, and off-road performance.

World War II (1939–1945)

In WWII, the auto industry *became* the war machine. Civilian car production stopped entirely. Factories switched to building tanks, bombers, and jeeps.

Economy

Ford’s Willow Run plant produced one B-24 bomber every hour by 1944. GM built over 850,000 military trucks. Civilian car manufacturing was banned from 1942 to 1945.

Roads & Urbanization

New roads and highways were built with military logistics in mind — shaping postwar transportation forever. Detroit earned the nickname “The Arsenal of Democracy.”

American Families

Rosie the Riveter represented the 350,000+ women who worked in auto plants. For the first time, women assembled engines and armored vehicles.

Families learned to keep and maintain old cars, sharing rides, and adapting — a preview of postwar consumer pragmatism.

Environment

Wartime production polluted heavily, but the drastic drop in civilian driving improved air quality in many industrial cities — an unexpected positive side effect.

📜 Restranscription of a dialogue with a former automotive technician

Me:Can you tell me about American vehicles used during World War I?
Christophe: Yes. The U.S. entered the war in 1917, and the car industry quickly shifted to military production. Companies like Ford, Packard, and Dodge were key players.
Me: What kind of vehicles were made?
Christophe: Mostly trucks, ambulances, and transport cars. The Ford Model T was adapted into an ambulance and a light transport vehicle. It was cheap, simple, easy to repair.
Me: Any special models?
Christophe: The Liberty Truck. It was a standard military truck, developed by the U.S. Army with input from different manufacturers. Around 9,000 were built during the war.
Me: Did you ever see these vehicles?
Christophe: I saw a restored Liberty Truck at the Gazoline Festival in Lamotte-Beuvron in 2019. Beautiful restoration. The owner told me it had been found in a barn in Ohio.
Me: Were these vehicles used only in Europe?
Christophe: Mostly, yes. They were shipped overseas to support the American Expeditionary Forces. Some stayed in Europe after the war. Others came back or were sold as surplus.
Me: What happened to the car industry after the war?
Christophe: Many factories returned to civilian production. But the war changed everything—mass production was faster, logistics better. The war helped shape the modern American auto industry.
